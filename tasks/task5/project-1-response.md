Phil: https://philippe-bn.github.io/cart263/projects/project-1/

Phil’s program consists of a blue square filled with many fish swimming in random directions, each varying in colour and size. As they move, the fish change direction whenever they collide with one another, creating a dynamic and constantly shifting pattern of motion. When the user moves their mouse over the blue square, the fish flock toward the cursor, as if drawn to its presence. However, when the mouse remains still, the fish gradually return to their previous behaviour, dispersing and exploring the space independently. At first, I noticed the simplicity of the program and appreciated the smooth movement of the fish, though I initially felt as if something was missing. As I continued watching, the motion began to feel more expressive, giving the fish a sense of personality and life within the code. I started to imagine my cursor as a kind of bait, attracting the fish as though they were gathering for a meal. Interestingly, my second impression of the fish was that they resembled small nukes, an unusual association, but one that became intriguing to reflect on. Speaking with Phil about the program deepened my understanding of his intentions. He passionately explained what he had learned, fascinated by how code could create something that seems to take on a life of its own.

Nerly & team: https://yaxuanpang.github.io/cart263/topics/project1/

The program is a recreation of the classic game Snake, built using an invisible grid that organizes the placement of the snake, food, and obstacles. Its vibrant colours stand out against the black background, evoking the aesthetic of early 2000s games. The animated background of moving circles and squares adds an extra layer of liveliness, making the project feel more detailed and thoughtfully designed. The instructions are clear and easy to follow, with touches of humour that lighten the experience, especially when the player fails. Although the game itself is a familiar concept, playing it in class brought a new level of enjoyment. It became a shared experience, where peers gathered around to watch and support one another. At one point, everyone near my friend watched anxiously as he got closer to beating the game. When he finally completed the last level, we all celebrated together, showing how simple games like this can foster a strong sense of community.

Diana: https://dianaedvi.github.io/CART-263/projects/project_1/

The program presents a tile set of unique puzzles that challenge the user in a variety of ways. In the background, cartoon-style ducks drift diagonally across the screen in a parallax effect, an addition I found both charming and unique. Overall, the program feels playful and vibrant, with visuals that are very pleasing to the eye. Most of the puzzles were relatively easy to solve, with the exception of the word puzzle, which I still found difficult to understand and decode. My favourite puzzle was the colour matching challenge, as it encouraged me to draw on my knowledge of colour theory and critical thinking. The RGB sliding mechanic felt especially clever and full of potential. I was particularly drawn to the idea of a colour-based game where users mix ingredients to achieve a specific result.
